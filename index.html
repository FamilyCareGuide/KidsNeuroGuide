What to Expect When You Go to a Neurologist

A friendly guide to help kids understand what may happen at a pediatric neurologist appointment, from talking with the doctor to the neurological exam, special tests, and next steps.

Start the guide Download PDF

This guide is for education only and is not medical advice. Always follow the instructions from your doctor or care team.

A neurology visit can feel less scary when you know what to expect.

Visiting a neurologist is a way to learn how your brain, nerves, and muscles are working. Most of the visit involves talking, simple movements, and activities that help the doctor understand your body.

Step 1

Talking With the Doctor

The appointment usually starts with the neurologist asking questions. They might ask why you are there, when your symptoms started, whether anything makes them better or worse, and whether you have been sick or injured recently.

They will also ask your parent or guardian about your health history and whether anyone else in your family has similar problems. Do not be afraid to answer honestly or speak up if something does not feel right. The more information you share, the easier it is for the neurologist to understand what is going on and help you.

Step 2

The Neurological Exam

A neurological exam is a series of simple activities to see how your brain and nerves are working. The doctor may ask you to follow a light with your eyes, smile, squeeze their hands, push or pull with your arms and legs, walk across the room, touch your finger to your nose, or feel a soft object touching your skin.

The doctor may also tap your knees with a small rubber hammer to check your reflexes. Most of these activities feel like simple games and do not hurt.

Step 3

Special Tests

Sometimes the neurologist needs more information. They might order an MRI to take pictures of your brain or spine, an EEG to measure brain activity with small stickers on your head, an EMG or nerve test to see how muscles and nerves work, or blood tests to look for other causes of symptoms.

To feel calmer, you can take slow deep breaths, listen to music, watch a movie if allowed, or bring a comfort item. It can also help to ask what each test is for and how it will help your doctor.

Step 4

After the Visit

Before you leave, the neurologist will talk with you and your parent or guardian about what they found. Sometimes they can explain what is causing your symptoms right away. Other times, they may need to wait for test results before making a diagnosis.

The neurologist will also explain the next steps. They may prescribe medicine, recommend therapy, order more tests, or schedule a follow-up appointment. This is a good time to ask questions so you and your family understand what happens next.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will the appointment hurt?

Most of the visit does not hurt. The doctor usually talks with you and asks you to do simple movements. Some tests may feel unusual, but your care team can explain what will happen before they begin.

Can I ask questions?

Yes. You can ask questions at any time. It is also okay to say if you feel nervous, confused, or uncomfortable. It can even be helpful to write down your questions before your appointment so you do not forget anything you want to ask.

What should I bring?

Bring a list of symptoms, questions, medicines, previous test results if you have them, and something comforting like a stuffed animal, book, or headphones.

Why might I need tests?

Tests can help the neurologist learn more about your brain, nerves, muscles, or symptoms. Not every child needs the same tests.
